New-Age Firms & BRSR: Early Lessons from Zomato, Nykaa, Paytm, etc.

“We’re trying to submit our BRSR, but honestly, we don’t even know where our Scope 3 emissions begin.”

— Sustainability Analyst, Indian tech startup (off the record)

By Anushka Joshi

The Context: ESG Just Got Real in India

India’s new digital-age companies – food-delivery platforms, fintechs, e‑commerce startups – are now subject to SEBI’s Business Responsibility & Sustainability Reporting (BRSR) mandate. As they publish BRSR sections or standalone sustainability reports for FY2023–24, common patterns and pain points are emerging. On the positive side, several companies are piloting innovative ESG initiatives; on the challenging side, gaps in data and process remain glaring.

Data Quality & Consistency Issues

Many disclosures note they’re still “early days” of BRSR, with inconsistencies in data quality and reporting standards persisting.

  • Paytm: Energy & emissions data, but FY2022–23 Scope 3 was “Not Tracked.”
  • Different companies use different units or boundaries, making benchmarking hard.
  • Nykaa: Discloses no Scope 3 figure or reduction targets at all.

Scope 3 and Other Emissions Gaps

Scope 1–2 are mostly covered, but most Scope 3 categories are not yet. This gap is acute for platform businesses with huge “user and partner” emissions (rider vehicles, data centers, shipping).

  • Food-delivery and mobility apps have significant indirect emissions.
  • Many companies offer EV plans but do not quantify associated GHG savings yet.
  • Disclosures from Zomato or Paytm note limited Scope 3 coverage, often only basics like employee travel.
  • Until Scope 3 accounting is standardized, this will remain a big blind spot.

Governance Structures: Traditional CSR vs ESG

Most new-age firms delegate ESG oversight to existing CSR or risk committees, rather than dedicated sustainability boards.

  • Zomato: Board’s CSR committee oversees ESG matters, meeting semi-annually.
  • Paytm: CSR Committee is responsible for sustainability-related issues.
  • This suggests few startups have fully-baked ESG governance yet, repurposing CSR structures.
  • A gap remains in disclosing clear ESG KPIs at the Board or C-suite level.
  • Many smaller companies still lack dedicated ESG leaders.

The Invisible Workforce: Gig Economy Left Out

BRSR’s social metrics are geared to “employees and workers,” but digital firms have large non-employee workforces.

“Delivery partners…have been considered as value-chain partners and hence are not a part of Zomato’s workforce.”

— Zomato BRSR, 2023–24

  • Most social metrics (diversity, training, welfare) only cover relatively small salaried staff.
  • Zomato’s corporate employees are ~4,000, while delivery partners number in the hundreds of thousands.
  • This is a systemic gap: workers critical to the business are not visible in mandatory disclosures.

Industry-Specific Challenges

Digital service companies face unique constraints under the BRSR template.

  • Data Availability: Hard to consolidate environmental data (fuel, packaging) across millions of independent drivers/merchants.
  • Regulatory Fit: BRSR assumes physical plants; unclear for fintech apps or e-commerce packaging.
  • Global Operations: Disclosures may omit international scopes.
  • Emerging Metrics: No established norms for data-center energy or cloud carbon footprints.

The lack of sector-specific templates means some key impacts remain underreported.

Noteworthy Practices and Innovations

Despite the gaps, some new-age companies are pushing the envelope on ESG.

Zomato:

  • Project ZEAL: 400+ PwDs as delivery partners.
  • Maternity benefits & insurance for women riders.
  • ~19k delivery partners trained in first-aid.
  • Ambulance service expanded to 500 cities.
  • Committed to 100% EV deliveries by 2030 (EV100).

Paytm:

  • Publishes BRSR online.
  • Has standing ESG team reporting to the CEO.

Nykaa:

  • Highlights sustainable sourcing (recycled plastics).
  • Built an ESG microsite for stakeholders.

Conclusion: From Reporting Headaches to Business Opportunity

The picture that emerges is one of partial compliance and growing pains. Data collection is ad hoc, metrics not standardized, and crucial categories (Scope 3, gig-social indicators) are treated as afterthoughts.

🌟 This creates a huge business opportunity:

Imagine a tech-enabled ESG data-management platform that:

  • Provides automated data pipelines (e.g., from billing/fuel logs).
  • Includes supply-chain databases (standard emission factors).
  • Offers modules for gig workforce tracking.
  • Features dashboard visualizations.
  • Generates templated reports aligned with SEBI’s format.
  • Leverages AI/analytics to flag inconsistencies and guide narrative disclosures.

This "sustainability data backbone" could help firms turn disjointed ESG metrics into polished, BRSR-compliant disclosures.
